Exploring Russian Interference Tactics in the 2016 U.S. Elections

Network of Russian Twitter bots (red) mentioning real accounts (green) and one another from 2014-2017.

Almost a year after taking office, President Donald Trump retweeted a Russian bot. This retweet was of a fake account called @ten_gop that posed as the “Unofficial Twitter of Tennessee Republicans.” Before being removed, it was favorited by nearly 45,000 people and retweeted by thousands more before it was eventually removed. At the time this tweet was published in 2017, the American public had already been informed about Russian interference in the 2016 presidential elections. How, then, could this fake account succeed- and consequently, American democracy fail- so dramatically?

Source: ThinkProgress

Since 2016, information about Russian interference in the presidential election has become public knowledge. What’s less obvious is the targeted strategies Russian actors used to achieve remarkable success in hacking American trust. In this article, we take a deep dive into the subtle strategies used by Russian trolls to push and pull our dialogue during the elections. By understanding how these troll accounts worked, particularly the successful ones, we seek to understand the biggest vulnerabilities in American digital trust, as well as give insight into how we can defend against such interference in upcoming elections.

Setup Strategies

The largest success of the troll accounts was establishing a voice. In the world of Twitter, being heard requires establishing authenticity as a run-of-the-mill neighbor, or authority as something like a news source. The accounts identified as trolls were able to do both with frightening success, using two main strategies.

1. Establishing Authenticity

The first strategy we observed in identified troll accounts was establishing authenticity as an average American citizen with an average American voice. Russian bots used varying rhetorical strategies over time to mimic the American voice and interject divisive dialogue. In the following wordclouds, we walk through the most frequently used hashtags in 2014, 2015, 2016, and 2017.

In 2014, just before election activity begins to stir, we see Russian fake accounts starting to establish themselves as authentic American accounts. Hashtags such as #Love and #USA dominate tweets in this year, as well as interesting references to music (#Itunes, #rap, #RAPCORE), famous Americans (#Kurt_Vonnegut, #SteveJobs), and emotions (#happy, #sadness, #lonely). This early setup stage— two years before the 2016 elections— indicates significant and intentional forethought on the part of the Russians as they prepared their bots to later inject divisive dialogue during the U.S. presidential election.

In 2015, we see the Russian bots begin to publish increasingly politically charged tweets, masquerading as Americans as they react to hot topics and news in the U.S. (#Prayers4California, #GOPDebate, #SanBernardino).

In 2016, the Russian bots peak in their divisive rhetoric, frequently supporting Donald Trump (#maga, #Trump2016) and antagonizing Hillary Clinton (#NeverHillary, #CrookedHillary).

Then, even after President Trump had won the 2016 elections, Russian bots continued to pose as Americans. In 2017, we see these fake accounts slowing down their political rhetoric and instead interjecting tweets about everyday American topics— similar to the 2014 setup stage. Alarmingly, this suggests that Russian actors sought to preserve the perceived authenticity of these fake accounts in preparation for more interference attempts in the future. The U.S. must prepare to prevent this from happening once more.

2. Establishing Authority

In addition to establishing the American authenticity of these fake accounts, Russian actors also sought to establish authority and power by garnering followers and posing as powerful American institutions.

When looking at user popularity (in favorites vs. followers), most troll tweets fall into a dense column near the y-axis; these posts garner a lot of favorites, despite being made by users with not many followers. Many of these accounts pose as regular people, with handles like @KarenParker93, @MsM0re, and @RealRobert1987.

However, we also see a band of accounts along the x-axis that do not have very popular posts, but do have many followers- and post quite a lot. This is a striking departure from where most accounts cluster along the y-axis. Interestingly, these low-favorited, well-followed accounts are almost all fake news accounts.

Examining account handles shows just how targeted fake news accounts were. Many mentioned densely-populated American cities, like @TodayNYCity. Some even copied names of genuine news outlets but replaced the word “news” with “new,” as in @ChicagoDailyNew or @DetroitDailyNew. The high follower counts of these accounts testify to their success at impersonating news outlets.

Notice that @ChicagoDailyNew and @DetroitDailyNew have the exact same account description, aside from the location mentioned. These cookie-cutter fake news accounts provide a glimpse into the deeply systematized spread of misinformation that took place during election interference.

Legitimacy for Russian troll accounts was more than appearing American- it also meant appearing to be an authoritative source.

Active/Interference Strategies

We wanted to better understand the patterns of interaction from trolls to real users and among the trolls themselves. As a result, we set on a network graph analysis of the mentions from trolls to other users.



We initially visualized the full network using directed graphs. In the photo, we see trolls in red and real users in green. While trolls were around 200 accounts, the total number of accounts in the graph was around 14000 indicating a large impact per troll. Our next step was to filter accounts that were not mentioned frequently.

Here, we see an image of accounts that were mentioned by trolls at least 10 times. We start to see here a slightly different, and clearer, pattern compared to the initial visualization. This highlights that targeting from trolls was not random, but rather each troll (or group of trolls) focused on a certain set of topics and users, which created the clusters.

In the 3 main clusters shown, we see variability in the topics of the real accounts, size of clusters and troll ratio to real users. We found that cluster 1 includes a high number of accounts that are driven by social justice (or a certain ideology). While the overwhelming majority of the trolls showed clear connections to the GOP and Trump’s voter base, this cluster was the most neutral.

Cluster 2 has a clear high number of trolls compared to real users. In addition, it has the largest number of trolls out of all 3. This was a cluster that had more news accounts than the other ones. We believe trolls here worked on building skepticism around news, which required a high number of trolls. Additionally, as news accounts tweet often, mentioning them is more likely than mentioning individuals.

Cluster 3 included clear GOP supporters and Trump supporters and family members. Here, clearly the number of real users is far larger than trolls. It is unclear why that’s the reason, but one possibility is that trolls worked on amplifying the message of these real accounts, which did not require as many mentions, but rather possible retweets and favorites.

While the 3 clusters are clearly separated, two of the accounts that are mentioned by trolls from all clusters are @realdonaldtrump and @hillaryclinton (they’re also the most mentioned accounts overall).

This clustering and network makes it clear that the trolls tried to imitate real people instead of solely clustering around candidates. In order to further investigate the trolls actions, we took a look at the network graph without real users.

One thing becomes clear right away here: most trolls did not interact with other trolls. In fact, we have a central weakly connected component that includes only 27% of the accounts, but more than 95% of the edges. One of the nodes that becomes immediately interesting as we look more at this graph is @ten_gop.

This is an account that @realdonaldtrump retweeted, and it is clearly well connected with other trolls (the most mentioned troll by other trolls). However, in all of the larger networks that we looked at, @ten_gop did not seem to be of particular significance.

On the contrary, when we visualize the full graph, we see a new special troll account. The account is @ameliebaldwin, and it’s in fact the most connected troll. We notice here that @ameliebaldwin’s style involves mentioning a lot of real users (many of which are mentioned only once). Other trolls follow a similar pattern, but @ameliebaldwin is the clear front-runner here. This is an interesting contrast to the case of @ten_gop. @ameliebaldwin is not significant in the trolls only network, but it interacts with real users way more. On the contrary, @ten_gop is a source of disinformation even for other trolls.

Digging into the Tweets

Even the frequency of their activity differs vastly; @ten_gop posted regularly and consistently, while @ameliebaldwin remained dormant for a long time and suddenly tweeted en masse.

Final Thoughts

The Russian attack on U.S. elections was sophisticated and consequential, leading to interactions with millions online who believed these bots were their fellow citizens. Perhaps most importantly, Russian interference continued after President Trump was sworn into office, and is likely happening to this very day. This calls for serious efforts on part of all social media platforms and the U.S. government to expect and defend against Russian interference in our upcoming 2020 presidential election. All of us can do our part too. By understanding the way Russian trolls behave and acknowledging their existence in our Twitter, Facebook, and Reddit feeds, we can all take a step in reading online opinions with caution, and reporting any accounts that seem suspicious.

Hopefully, we will be better prepared this time.
